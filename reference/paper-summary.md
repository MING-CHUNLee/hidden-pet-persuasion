# Paper Summary: The Pet Exposure Effect

**Title:** The Pet Exposure Effect: Exploring the Differential Impact of Dogs Versus Cats on Consumer Mindsets
**Authors:** Lei Jia, Xiaojing Yang, Yuwei Jiang
**Journal:** Journal of Marketing, 2022, Vol. 86(5), pp. 42-57
**DOI:** 10.1177/00222429221078036

---

## Core Thesis

Exposure to dogs activates a **promotion-focused** mindset (eager, risk-seeking, gain-oriented), while exposure to cats activates a **prevention-focused** mindset (vigilant, risk-averse, loss-avoiding). This effect carries over to **pet-unrelated** consumer decisions.

## Theoretical Foundation: Regulatory Focus Theory (Higgins, 1997)

| Dimension | Promotion Focus | Prevention Focus |
|-----------|----------------|-----------------|
| Goal | Advancement, growth, gains | Safety, security, avoiding losses |
| Strategy | Eager approach | Vigilant avoidance |
| Sensitivity | Presence/absence of positive outcomes | Presence/absence of negative outcomes |
| Pet association | **Dogs** (enthusiastic, eager, adaptable) | **Cats** (cautious, vigilant, territorial) |

## Mechanism

1. **Pet stereotypes as primes:** Dogs are stereotypically perceived as enthusiastic, eager-to-please, and adaptable; cats as cautious, independent, and vigilant.
2. **Trait transfer:** Exposure to pets (even via pictures, videos, or recall) activates the stereotypical traits associated with that species.
3. **Mindset activation:** These activated traits prime a corresponding regulatory focus (promotion vs. prevention).
4. **Downstream spillover:** The activated mindset carries over to influence subsequent, pet-unrelated consumer judgments and behaviors.

## Key Studies & Findings

### Study 1 — Secondary Data (Google Trends + COVID-19)
- States with higher % of **dog owners** → more Google searches for promotion-focused words + higher COVID-19 transmission rates (riskier behavior).
- States with higher % of **cat owners** → more Google searches for prevention-focused words.

### Study 2 — Financial Risk-Taking
- **Manipulation:** Participants recalled past experience with a dog or cat.
- **DV:** Investment allocation between a risky stock fund and a safe money market.
- **Result:** Dog-exposed participants allocated significantly more to risky investments vs. cat-exposed participants.
- **Mediator:** Promotion vs. prevention focus (measured via RFQ).

### Study 3 (a–d) — Product Preferences Across Categories
- Multiple product categories tested (sunscreen, hand sanitizer, vitamins, sneakers).
- Dog exposure → preference for **promotion-framed** products (e.g., "boost energy," "enhance performance").
- Cat exposure → preference for **prevention-framed** products (e.g., "reduce risk," "prevent damage").
- Studies 3c & 3d used real purchase behavior (not just stated preference).

### Study 4 — Pet Stereotypicality as Moderator
- **Key finding:** The effect only works when pets conform to species stereotypes.
- A stereotypical dog (enthusiastic, eager) → promotion focus ✓
- An atypical dog (aloof, cautious) → effect disappears ✗
- A stereotypical cat (cautious, vigilant) → prevention focus ✓
- An atypical cat (overly friendly, eager) → effect disappears ✗
- **Implication for design:** Pet stimuli must evoke stereotypical traits to trigger the effect.

### Study 5 — Regulatory Fit in Advertising (Massage Service)
- Pet exposure (dog vs. cat) × Ad framing (promotion vs. prevention).
- **Regulatory fit effect:** Dog exposure + promotion-framed ad → highest purchase intention. Cat exposure + prevention-framed ad → highest purchase intention.
- Mediated by "feeling right" (regulatory fit).

### Study 6 — Real Bidding Behavior (Sneakers)
- Used actual monetary bids in an auction.
- Dog exposure + promotion-focused sneaker ad → higher bids.
- Cat exposure + prevention-focused sneaker ad → higher bids.
- Mediated by regulatory fit.

### Study 7 — Within-Subject Design (Toothpaste)
- Participants evaluated two toothpaste products (one promotion-framed, one prevention-framed).
- Dog-exposed → preferred promotion-framed toothpaste (M = 7.41 vs. 6.29).
- Cat-exposed → preferred prevention-framed toothpaste (M = 6.94 vs. 6.45).
- Regulatory fit confirmed as mediator.

## Boundary Conditions & Moderators

1. **Pet stereotypicality** (Study 4): Effect disappears when pet does not match species stereotypes.
2. **Exposure modality is flexible:** Effect works across recalled experiences, pictures, videos, and ads.
3. **No pet ownership required:** Even non-owners are affected by pet exposure.

## Practical Implications (from the paper)

- Marketers should use **dogs** in ads for promotion-focused products (stocks, sports cars, energy drinks).
- Marketers should use **cats** in ads for prevention-focused products (insurance, mutual funds, safety equipment).
- Pet stereotypical traits must be salient in the messaging for the effect to work.
- Big data (pet ownership, pet video viewing) can inform targeted marketing strategy.
